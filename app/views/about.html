Our Principles

Free and open source - AGPLv3

Integrate with open standards (via Poplus, PopIt, Popolo)

100% financially transparent

100% sustained on donations and grant-funding

Never have advertisements

Never sell your data

Never collect a fee from candidates or users

Transparent design and development process

Dedicated to preventing a monopoly from forming over public election data

Collaborate with and support as many free & open source civic technology projects as possible

Features

Learn about, connect with, and share your opinion on all of your candidates in one convenient place online

Create a list of candidates you are voting for, and share your list over social media. Make a difference beyond your vote by advocating for candidates to the people you care about.

Supported on computer, tablet, and mobile devices

Why university/K-12 elections?

Students have a vital role to play in the evolution and development of election information and debate technology. Across the world there are 10,000s of student elections held each year, and every office tends to be up for reelection annually. Each of these elections is an opportuntiy to familiarize students with the latest and greatest in online civic engagement tools, and an opportunity to discover the effectiveness and weaknesses of our existing civic technology.

Due to the frequency and diversity, yet relative simplicity of student elections, we can build, test, listen to feedback, and reiterate tools for student elections much faster than we can than if we wait for government election cycles.

College students are among the earliest adopters of internet technologies, and are not beholden to financial donors or mainstream party politics like government candidates. Therefore, student candidates may be more open to trying technologies that increase their transparency, accessibility, and accountability to the public.

Better than any other demographic, students can lead the development and demonstrate the potential for internet technology to improve election participation and civic engagement.

Popolo - "[We] define data interchange formats and data models so that organizations can spend less time transforming and modeling data and more time applying it to the problems they face.
